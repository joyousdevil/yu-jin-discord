# THE BUTTERFLY LANDS

*A Cyberpunk Red Romance Narrative for Yu-Jin "Nabi" Kim*

***

> *"The perilous journey was a labor not of attainment but of reattainment, not discovery but rediscovery. The godly powers sought and dangerously won are revealed to have been within the heart of the hero all the time."*
> — Joseph Campbell, *The Hero with a Thousand Faces*

***

## PART ONE — DEPARTURE

### I. A Good Life

Yu-jin Kim has a system.

She wakes at six. She showers in water that's never quite hot enough because the building's heater runs on optimism and failing infrastructure. She dresses in the dark — fitted jeans, cream jacket, boots with worn heels that know every crack in Japantown's sidewalks — and she's at Halmae's by six-forty, where Mrs. Bae has already set out a bowl of juk and a look of maternal disapproval.

"You look tired," Mrs. Bae says. She says this every morning. Yu-jin has looked tired since she was nineteen.

"I slept fine."

"You slept four hours. I can hear your light through the floor."

"Your hearing is medically improbable, Mrs. Bae."

"My hearing is a mother's hearing. Eat your porridge."

Yu-jin eats her porridge. She opens the notebook — leather-bound, rubber-banded, irreplaceable — and she reviews the day. Three supplier meetings. Two favor callbacks. One Night Market to organize. A Tyger Claw lieutenant who wants first pick of the medical supplies, and a Watson clinic that needs those same supplies more but can pay less.

She solves this in four minutes, with two calls and a text that reads: *I know a guy. Actually, I know four guys, a woman, and a cooperative. Let me make some calls.*

The lieutenant gets second pick but at a discount. The clinic gets first pick and a payment plan. Both of them owe her. Neither of them knows about the other's deal. Everyone eats.

This is the system. It works.

By noon, she's brokered six connections, updated forty-three entries in the notebook, eaten lunch standing up outside a convenience store in Watson, and told exactly zero people how she's feeling, because feelings are maintenance, and Yu-jin doesn't have time for maintenance.

By midnight, she's home. The apartment smells like sesame oil and jasmine. She watches twenty minutes of *Love in the Time of Chrome* — a reality dating show so terrible it's achieved a kind of anti-art grandeur — while updating her ledger. She brushes her teeth. She checks the locks twice.

She goes to bed alone in an apartment that is clean and quiet and organized and hers.

It is a good life. She built it from nothing, with a pen and a dead man's methodology and the specific kind of stubbornness that runs in families where the women outlive the systems that were supposed to protect them.

It is a good life, and it is a small life, and she is aware of both facts simultaneously, the way you're aware of a window that faces a wall — you know there's a city out there, you can hear it, but the view is what it is.

She doesn't need anyone.

She has forty businesses that need her, three clinics that depend on her, and a grandmother in Busan who sends kimchi through Nomad couriers and calls every Wednesday to ask if she's eating properly.

She's fine.

She's *fine*.

***

### II. The Nomad

The first time Yu-jin sees Gram Ryder, he's standing in front of Halmae's looking like a man who got lost on his way to somewhere that doesn't exist anymore.

He's tall. Beard. Nomad leathers that fit like a second skin, road-worn and sun-faded in the way that means he didn't buy them — he *grew* into them. He's looking at the menu posted on the window with the expression of a man who is trying to figure out if he can afford to eat today and is calculating whether dignity or hunger will win.

Hunger wins. It almost always does.

He comes inside. Mrs. Bae seats him by the window, because Mrs. Bae seats everyone by the window — her theory is that natural light makes people order more — and Yu-jin doesn't pay attention, because she's working, and strangers come into Halmae's every day, and most of them want something, and she'll find out what it is eventually.

Except he doesn't want anything.

He orders bibimbap. He eats it quietly, with the careful concentration of someone who understands that food is not guaranteed. He thanks Mrs. Bae in a voice that's low and Southern-warm, the kind of voice that makes "thank you, ma'am" sound like it means something, and he leaves a tip that's too large for someone whose boots are that worn.

Yu-jin notices the tip. She notices everything — it's her job, it's her pathology, it's the thing her father taught her before he taught her anything else. *Everyone has something they need. Find it, and you'll never be without friends.*

She files the information: Nomad. Alone. Polite. Either generous or performing generosity, and she can't tell which, and that bothers her, because she can always tell.

He comes back two days later. Orders the same thing. Thanks Mrs. Bae the same way. This time, on his way out, he stops at Yu-jin's table — not because he knows who she is, not because he has an angle, but because he needs directions to a parts shop for his truck.

"Do you know where I can find a decent mechanic? Not the kind that sells your parts back to you."

She gives him directions. He says, "Thank you, ma'am," again, and she is twenty-seven years old and no one has ever called her ma'am with that particular frequency of earnestness, and she doesn't know what to do with it, so she does what she always does with information she can't categorize.

She writes it in the notebook.

*Nomad. Name unknown. Polite. Looking for mechanic. Referred to Kim's Auto, Watson. Good tipper. Comes back.*

She doesn't know why she writes that last part. People come back to Halmae's all the time. Mrs. Bae's jjigae is famous in six districts.

But she writes it anyway.

Comes back.

***

### III. I'm Fine

Here is what Yu-jin knows about depending on people:

Her father depended on Kang Tao. He documented everything, filed the right reports, maintained the right records, and trusted that the system would protect him. They found him in a warehouse in Santo Domingo with three other employees, all dead, all undocumented, all forgotten within seventy-two hours.

Her mother depended on her father. Twelve time zones of video calls and quarterly visits and careful maintenance, and then one day the maintenance cost more than the connection was worth, and the divorce happened quietly, the way things end when no one has the energy to fight.

Yu-jin depends on her notebook. Paper doesn't betray you. Paper doesn't disappear into a warehouse. Paper doesn't file for divorce across twelve time zones.

So when Gram Ryder — Deacon, she learns, they call him Deacon — comes back a third time, and this time brings a container of kimchi jjigae that he leaves with Mrs. Bae because *"I heard she's been working through meals again and that's a shame"* — Yu-jin does not feel her chest tighten.

She does not feel anything.

She is a professional. She is a Fixer. She reads people for a living and she has read this man and he is exactly what he appears to be — kind without performing it — and the simplicity of that is not, *is not*, making her chest hurt in a way she can't file in any notebook.

"He didn't wait to be thanked," Mrs. Bae observes, drying a bowl. "He didn't even mention it."

"I noticed."

"Most men who bring food want you to know they brought food."

"I said I noticed, Mrs. Bae."

Mrs. Bae smiles the way she smiles when she's about to say something that will land in about six hours. "You know, your father was the same way. Quiet about kindness. Loud about nothing."

Yu-jin puts down her pen. "Don't."

"Don't what?"

"Don't — do the thing."

"What thing?"

"The thing where you compare a stranger to my father because he brought me soup."

"He brought *me* soup. For *you*. There's a difference." Mrs. Bae sets the bowl on the drying rack. "Also, he fixed my dishwasher."

"… What?"

"Last Tuesday. It was leaking. He noticed. He came back with tools and fixed it. Took him twenty minutes. I offered to pay him and he looked at me like I'd insulted his family."

Yu-jin stares at the old woman. Mrs. Bae stares back.

"He's a Nomad," Yu-jin says, as though this is a counterargument.

"He's a person who fixes things without being asked. In my experience, that's rarer than a Nomad."

Yu-jin picks up her pen. Goes back to the notebook. Files the information where it belongs: in the growing, increasingly inconvenient section of her brain labeled *Things About Deacon That Don't Fit Any Category She Has*.

She's fine.

She's *fine*.

***

### IV. Everyone Has Opinions

Three weeks after the dishwasher incident, Mrs. Bae invites Deacon to sit at the counter during off-hours. This is notable because Mrs. Bae doesn't invite anyone to sit at the counter. The counter is Mrs. Bae's domain — it's where she chops vegetables and judges humanity and occasionally threatens Tyger Claws with a soup ladle.

But Deacon gets the counter. And tea.

"Because he fixed my dishwasher," Mrs. Bae explains.

"You could have hired someone."

"I could have. But he was already here, and he didn't ask for anything, and the last time I hired someone they charged me two hundred eddies and the dishwasher leaked worse."

"That's not—"

"Also, he reminds me of my husband."

Yu-jin's pen stops. Mrs. Bae's husband was a fixer in the 2020s. He's been dead for twenty years. Mrs. Bae does not talk about him lightly.

"Your husband was a Nomad?"

"My husband was a man who paid attention. Who walked into a room and noticed what was broken before he noticed what was shiny." She dries her hands on her apron. "That's not common. In men or in anyone."

Yu-jin waits.

Mrs. Bae doesn't continue. She goes back to chopping onions with the precise violence of a woman who has been handling knives longer than most people have been alive.

That evening, Yu-jin finds herself sitting one table closer to the counter than usual. Not on purpose. The light's just better there.

***

The crew forms the way most things form in Night City — accidentally, reluctantly, and because someone needed something and someone else had it.

Vincent brings the planning. Galen brings the medicine and the jokes. Deacon brings the driving and the quiet competence and the way he checks on people without being asked — *everyone*, not just the ones who can pay him back.

And Yu-jin brings the connections. The work. The reason they're all in the same room.

She keeps it professional. She gives Deacon jobs that require his particular skills — smuggling runs, extraction driving, logistics that need someone who knows roads the way netrunners know code. She pays fairly, communicates clearly, maintains appropriate boundaries.

She does not bring extra food to crew meetings.

Except she does. But she frames it as leftovers from Halmae's. *"Mrs. Bae made too much again, I don't want it to go to waste."* This is a lie. Mrs. Bae has never in sixty-eight years of cooking made too much of anything. Mrs. Bae portions with the precision of a military quartermaster.

She does not create excuses to be in Deacon's proximity.

Except she does. *"I need to inspect HAUS's cargo capacity for the Night Market transport logistics."* This is technically true. It is also technically unnecessary, because she inspected it last week.

She does not notice the way his voice drops half a register when he's being serious, or the way his hands look on a steering wheel, or the way he maintains his vehicles like they're family members who happen to have engines.

She notices all of it. She files it in the mental notebook she'll never show anyone. The one that doesn't have a label, because labeling it would make it real, and real things in Night City have a way of ending up in warehouses in Santo Domingo.

***

Galen figures it out first.

Because of course he does. Galen reads people the way he reads wounds — clinically, accurately, and with a running commentary that nobody asked for.

"So," Galen says, leaning against the wall of the cargo container while Deacon works under HAUS's hood and Yu-jin definitely isn't watching. "How long are we doing this?"

"Doing what?"

"The thing where you bring food to a man who doesn't know you're bringing food *for him specifically* and then stand four meters away pretending to read your notebook."

Yu-jin's expression doesn't change. "I'm doing supply chain logistics."

"You're watching him adjust a carburetor."

"It's relevant to transport efficiency."

"It's relevant to the fact that you've been holding that page open for twelve minutes and you haven't written anything."

Yu-jin closes the notebook. Slowly. With the kind of deliberate control that means she's considering several responses, all of them devastating, and is choosing the most surgical one.

"Galen."

"Yes?"

"If you continue this line of conversation, I will reassign you to the Pacifica clinic run, which as you know involves a three-hour drive through Scav territory with no bathroom stops."

Galen raises his hands. "Copy that. Totally misread the situation. No further observations."

He walks away. Pauses. Turns back.

"Just so you know — he watches you too. When you're not looking. He does this thing where he checks to make sure you've eaten before he eats."

"Galen."

"Pacifica. Got it. Shutting up."

He doesn't shut up. He will never shut up. But he lets it rest, because even Galen knows that some truths need time to become comfortable, and Yu-jin is the kind of person who meets the truth on her own terms or not at all.

***

## PART TWO — INITIATION

### V. I Really Wanna Stay At Your House

The first real moment happens at two in the morning on a Tuesday, and it is not romantic. It is not cinematic. It is a woman cooking doenjang jjigae in a tiny apartment above a noodle shop because the world got too heavy and the stove is the only thing that makes it lighter.

Yu-jin cooks when she's stressed. She's always cooked when she's stressed — it's her mother's kitchen in Busan compressed into a Japantown studio, the smell of fermented soybean paste filling a space that's too small for the feeling it's trying to hold.

Tonight, the stress has a name: Kang Tao. Another recruiter. Another salary offer. Another benefits package wrapped in corporate silk that's really a leash with a brand name.

She said no. She always says no. But this time the recruiter smiled in a way that suggested future conversations might not involve smiling.

The jjigae is simmering. The apartment smells like home — not this home, the other one, the one with the heated floors and the grandmother who sends seaweed in vacuum-sealed containers. Yu-jin is stirring the pot and not crying, because she doesn't cry, because crying is maintenance, because—

There's a knock at the door.

It's soft. Careful. The knock of someone who knows it's two in the morning and is deeply aware that this is an unreasonable time to knock on doors.

She opens it with one hand on her phone, ready to call Park Ji-hoon if necessary, because Night City doesn't knock politely at two in the morning unless it wants something.

Deacon is standing in the hallway.

He's holding a carburetor.

"I'm sorry," he says immediately, and the apology is so sincere that it borders on structural. "I know it's late. I saw your light. Mrs. Bae gave me a key to the building because of the dishwasher thing, and I was working on HAUS in the lot, and I smelled — is that doenjang jjigae?"

"… Why are you holding a carburetor?"

He looks down at his hands. He's still holding the carburetor. He appears to have genuinely forgotten he was holding it.

"I — was working on HAUS. Heard — I heard your light was on." He pauses. Processes his own sentence. "I mean. I know you can't hear light. I saw your light. And I smelled something cooking. And I thought—" He stops. Starts again. "Mrs. Bae says you cook when things are bad."

A silence.

"Did Mrs. Bae send you?"

"Mrs. Bae doesn't send anyone anywhere. Mrs. Bae suggests with the force of a natural disaster and lets you think it was your idea."

Despite everything — the recruiter, the stress, the two AM crisis cooking — Yu-jin's mouth twitches.

"That's… accurate."

"I should go. I'm sorry. I just wanted to make sure you were—"

"Do you want soup?"

The question exits her mouth before she approves it. It bypasses every checkpoint, every firewall, every carefully maintained protocol for keeping people at the distance where they can't disappear on you.

Deacon looks at her. He looks at the carburetor. He looks at the hallway.

"I don't want to impose."

"You're standing in my hallway at two in the morning holding an engine component. We're past imposing."

That smile. The one that's annoyingly genuine. "Yes, ma'am."

He puts the carburetor down by the door. He takes off his boots without being asked — a small gesture that Yu-jin catalogs with the same intensity she catalogs everything, except this one lands somewhere in her ribcage instead of her notebook.

They eat jjigae at her kitchen table. He doesn't ask why she was cooking at two in the morning. She doesn't tell him. He eats the way he always eats — carefully, gratefully, like food is a gift and not a given. She eats the way she always eats — mechanically, efficiently, while updating mental files.

They talk about nothing. Supply routes. HAUS's engine problems. The fact that Galen somehow acquired a medical scanner that's technically NCPD evidence and is refusing to explain how. The terrible reality dating show that Yu-jin watches — Deacon, it turns out, has also seen it, because he gets exactly three channels in the cargo container and one of them is the channel that airs *Love in the Time of Chrome*.

"The rose ceremony is rigged," Deacon says, with the quiet conviction of a man stating an obvious truth.

"Of course it's rigged. Arasaka sponsors the show. They rig everything."

"But did you see the episode where the cyberpsycho declared his love through interpretive chrome-dance?"

"That was the best episode of television ever produced."

"It was *art*."

Yu-jin laughs. Not the polite, controlled laugh she uses in business. An actual laugh — short, surprised, the sound of a defense failing at a structural level. She covers her mouth with her hand, as though she can put it back.

Deacon grins. It's the grin of a man who has just heard something rare and knows it.

They do the dishes together. He washes, she dries. He doesn't comment on the framed photograph of her father on the wall. He doesn't ask about the locked case under the bed that she catches him not-looking at with the deliberate care of a man who respects closed doors.

At the door, he picks up his carburetor. She hands him a container of leftover jjigae. Their fingers brush on the lid.

"Thank you," he says. "For the soup."

"Thank you," she says. "For — checking."

He nods. Turns to go. Stops.

"Yu-jin?"

It's the first time he's used her name. Not Nabi, not the handle, not the Fixer-at-Halmae's. Her name.

"Yes?"

"Whatever's keeping you up at two in the morning — you don't have to handle it alone. That's not how this works."

"How what works?"

"Crew."

He says it simply. Like it's a fact. Like it's not the most terrifying word in her vocabulary — a word that means depending on people, which means giving them the coordinates to the places where you're soft, which means eventually, inevitably, standing in a warehouse that smells like consequences and realizing that crew doesn't keep you safe, nothing keeps you safe, nothing—

"Good night, Deacon."

"Good night, Yu-jin."

She closes the door. Leans against it. Presses her hand to her sternum, where something is happening that she doesn't have a page for.

She doesn't sleep. She sits at the kitchen table, in the seat he was sitting in, which is still warm, and she opens the notebook to the page about him.

She writes: *Came to check. No angle. No ask. Brought a carburetor by accident. Likes doenjang jjigae. Thinks the rose ceremony is rigged.*

She pauses. Writes one more line.

*Dangerous.*

***

### VI. Everybody Notices

The next four months are an exercise in what Galen later calls — in his toast at a gathering neither Yu-jin nor Deacon will admit is a date — *"the longest, most nutritionally dense courtship in Night City history."*

This is what happens:

Yu-jin brings food to crew meetings. This is normal. This is what she does. What is not normal is that the food is increasingly elaborate, increasingly Korean, and increasingly calibrated to one person's preferences. Deacon mentioned once — *once* — that he liked the japchae. The next meeting features japchae. And the meeting after that. And the one after that.

"Is japchae the only Korean dish?" Vincent asks, with the carefully neutral observation of a man who has noticed a pattern and is choosing his moment.

"Mrs. Bae had extra." This lie has been deployed fourteen times. It is becoming structurally unsound.

"Mrs. Bae had extra japchae for the third consecutive Wednesday?"

"She likes to batch cook."

Vincent says nothing. He writes something in his planner. Yu-jin, who can read upside down, catches the words *japchae frequency anomaly* before he closes it.

***

Deacon fixes things. This is also normal. Deacon fixes everything — it's his love language, the Nomad's vocabulary of care expressed through socket wrenches and engine oil. What is not normal is the specificity.

He fixes the loose step outside Halmae's. He fixes the flickering light in the Night Market stall that gives Yu-jin headaches. He fixes the broken lock on the supply closet in the Watson clinic that Yu-jin mentioned in passing, once, three weeks ago, in a conversation that was ostensibly about medical supply logistics.

"Did you fix my supply closet?" Yu-jin asks.

"It needed fixing."

"It wasn't your supply closet."

"It was broken. I fix broken things."

"You drove forty minutes to Watson to fix a lock I mentioned one time."

A pause. Deacon's ears, visible above the beard, turn slightly pink. This is new data. Yu-jin files it immediately.

"… The drive wasn't that bad. I needed to check a route anyway."

"The route to Watson runs through Japantown?"

"It does if you take the scenic route."

The scenic route through Japantown passes Halmae's. Yu-jin decides not to point this out. Instead, she says, "The clinic says thank you."

"Tell them it was nothing."

It wasn't nothing. They both know it wasn't nothing. But they let the fiction stand, because the fiction is load-bearing, and removing it would mean acknowledging the architecture underneath, and neither of them is ready for that floor plan.

***

The crew develops a betting pool.

Yu-jin doesn't know about this. She will find out later and there will be consequences. But for now, the pool runs in whispered exchanges:

Galen has money on "confession during a medical emergency," because Galen is a romantic who disguises it as cynicism.

Vincent has money on "twelve to eighteen months, confession initiated by Deacon, probably vehicular context," because Vincent is a man who believes in data and probabilities and the statistical inevitability of two people with compatible emotional frequencies converging on a mean.

Mrs. Bae has money on "before winter," because Mrs. Bae's husband courted her for exactly three months in 1998 and she considers this the correct timeline for all human romance. She also considers herself an active participant in this particular outcome.

Troy Santos, when told about the pool, stares blankly and says, "Gambling is technically—" before Galen puts a hand on his shoulder and says, "Troy. Please. Let people have this."

Troy's left thumb trembles once. He excuses himself.

(He later, through intermediary, puts money on "Yu-jin confesses first." When asked why, he says: "She's the one with the notebook. She'll figure it out before he does." This is, objectively, the most perceptive thing Troy Santos has ever said about human emotion. Galen considers it a breakthrough.)

***

### VII. The Butterfly Lands

It happens on a Night Market night.

Not the kind of night where everything goes wrong. Night City has plenty of those — bullets and corporate extraction and the particular flavor of adrenaline that tastes like copper and consequences. This isn't that.

This is worse.

This is a beautiful night. Clear sky. The Night Market is humming — vendors in their stalls, the smell of street food and ozone, the colored lights strung between buildings making Japantown look like someone's idea of home. Yu-jin is in her element, moving between stalls, checking on vendors, solving problems with the efficiency of a woman who has done this so many times it's muscle memory.

Deacon is working security with Ji-hoon. Not because he has to — he's not on the security roster — but because he volunteered, because of course he did, because there is a direct correlation between "things Yu-jin cares about" and "things Deacon shows up for" that is so statistically obvious that Vincent has started graphing it.

And then Yu-jin's phone rings.

It's Busan.

It's not Sunday, and it's not Wednesday, and her mother doesn't call outside schedule unless—

"Eomma?"

Her mother's voice is calm. Too calm. The calm of a woman who has practiced this sentence.

"Halmeoni fell. She's at the hospital. They're saying it's her hip."

The Night Market keeps humming. The lights keep glowing. The city keeps being a city.

Yu-jin goes quiet. Not her business quiet — the other kind. The kind she was at nineteen, sitting in an NCPD office while a detective didn't return her calls, learning that systems don't protect you and the truth is something you build leverage to demand.

She hangs up. She stands in the gap between two stalls, in the blind spot where no one can see her, and she presses her hands flat against the corrugated metal wall and she breathes.

Her grandmother is eighty-one. Her grandmother has heated floors and sends care packages. Her grandmother is the most dangerous person in her life — not because she's a threat, but because she's a vulnerability. The one place where Yu-jin's independence fails. The one person she can't protect with a notebook.

"Yu-jin?"

Deacon. Standing at the mouth of the alleyway. Not too close. He's learned the geography of her boundaries — where you can stand, where you can't, where the lines are.

"I'm fine."

"You're not."

"I need a minute."

"Okay."

He doesn't leave. He doesn't come closer. He stands exactly where he is, at the exact distance she needs, and he waits.

This is the thing she can't write in any notebook. The thing that doesn't fit any category. This man, this Nomad who lost his entire family and is still, *somehow*, standing in an alley in Japantown at the exact distance she needs, waiting with the patience of a person who understands that proximity is not the same as intrusion.

"My grandmother fell," she says. She doesn't know why she says it. She doesn't tell people things. She solves things. She manages things. She writes things in notebooks and makes calls and fixes the supply chain and makes sure everyone eats—

"Is she okay?"

"I don't know. My mother called. They're at the hospital."

"Do you need to go?"

"Go where? Busan? I can't—" She stops. The practicalities are a relief. "It's twelve time zones. There's nothing I can do from here."

"That's not what I asked."

She looks at him. Really looks. The way she looks at people when she's reading them, except she's not reading him — she's seeing him, which is different, which is harder.

"I need to stay. The Night Market—"

"Ji-hoon has the market. Galen's here. Vincent can coordinate remotely." He takes half a step closer. Not enough to crowd. Enough to be real. "What do *you* need?"

No one asks her that. Everyone asks her what *they* need — what shipment, what connection, what favor, what introduction. She is the person who makes sure everyone eats. She is not the person who is asked if she has eaten.

She can't answer. Her throat is doing something inconvenient.

"Come on," Deacon says. He tilts his head toward the lot where HAUS is parked. "I'll drive."

"Drive where?"

"Nowhere. Everywhere. Down the coast. Up the freeway. Whatever you need. Sometimes the best thing for a bad night is a long road."

It's such a Nomad answer. Such a profoundly Nomad answer — the idea that motion is medicine, that distance is therapy, that the thing you need when the world gets small is to make it big again.

She should say no. She should stay. She should manage the Night Market and maintain control and keep the system running because the system is all she has—

"Okay," she says.

***

They drive for three hours.

HAUS rumbles south along the coast, past the Badlands border, past the places where Night City's neon fades into darkness and the sky gets big enough to hold stars. The charm on the rearview mirror — braided cord, red and gold and black, Ryder Clan colors — swings gently with the motion.

Yu-jin doesn't talk. Deacon doesn't make her. He drives the way he does everything — steady, attentive, aware — and he plays the radio low, and it's some old Southern station that comes in static-fuzzy and warm, and the music sounds like highways and families who keep driving even when they don't know where they're going.

Somewhere past the Aldecaldo border markers, she starts talking.

She tells him about her grandmother. About the heated floors and the care packages and the notes in formal Korean that she keeps in a drawer tied with a rubber band. She tells him about her father's notebooks and the warehouse and the seventy-two hours. She tells him about the twenty-minute phone calls on Sundays — not because either of them wants them to be short, but because twenty minutes is the exact duration before the silence fills with all the things they're not saying.

She tells him about the locked case under her bed and how she's never gone through all the notebooks because some of those manifests may contain information worth more than she knows — worth dying for — and she doesn't know if she's more afraid of what she'll find or of the fact that finding it will make her father's death real in a way she's been carefully avoiding for eight years.

She tells him about independence. How it's her greatest strength and her greatest limitation. How she chose freedom and accepted vulnerability and trusted the network to catch her when she fell, but the truth is the network is *hers*, she built it, she maintains it, and if the butterfly stops flying, the whole thing comes apart.

"What do you do when the butterfly gets tired?" she asks, and she hates how small her voice sounds.

Deacon is quiet for a long time. The road hums. The charm swings.

"She lands," he says finally. "She finds a place to land."

"And if the place isn't safe?"

He looks at her. Just for a second, the way you glance at something you don't want to look away from.

"Then someone makes it safe."

They drive back. It's almost dawn. The sky is turning the color of bruised peaches over the Badlands, and Night City is materializing on the horizon like a fever dream reassembling itself.

Deacon pulls HAUS into the lot behind Halmae's. Kills the engine.

"Thank you," Yu-jin says. "For the road."

"Anytime."

She opens the door. Pauses. Looks at the charm on the rearview mirror.

"Deacon?"

"Yeah?"

"Your mother made that. You told Galen once. She said every vehicle needs a soul."

He looks at the charm. At her. Back at the charm.

"You remember that?"

"I remember everything. It's my job." She pauses. "It's also — not my job. This time."

Something moves across his face. Surprise. Warmth. The specific expression of a man who has just realized that someone has been paying attention to him in a way that has nothing to do with transactions.

She gets out of the truck before either of them has to figure out what that expression means.

***

### VIII. I Am Afraid

Two days later, Yu-jin's grandmother is fine. Hip bruised, not broken. The doctors are calling it a miracle and Halmeoni is calling it *"a fuss about nothing, tell Yu-jin I fell because the floor was wet, not because I'm old, and she should eat more."*

Yu-jin cries for the first time in three years. Alone. In her apartment. On the floor of the kitchen, which is ridiculous, she knows, but that's where her legs decide to stop working, and the tiles are warm because Mrs. Bae keeps the building heat running higher than is strictly necessary.

She cries for six minutes. Then she washes her face. Makes tea. Opens the notebook.

And stares at the page about Deacon.

The page has grown. It started as a single line — *Nomad. Name unknown. Polite.* — and it now spans four pages. Not the business information. She has a separate file for that. These four pages are the other kind: the observations, the details, the things she's noticed because noticing is her pathology and she can't turn it off.

The way he checks on people without being asked. The way he drove for three hours without talking because she needed silence. The way his voice drops half a register when he's being serious. The way he said *she lands* like it was obvious, like the butterfly landing wasn't the end of freedom but the beginning of something else.

She looks at the four pages and she understands, with the clinical precision of a woman who reads people for a living, that she is in love.

This is not news. This has not been news for weeks, possibly months. This has been building the way all structural failures build — slowly, invisibly, in the places you're not watching.

The problem isn't the love. The problem is what love requires.

Love requires landing. Love requires letting someone see the locked case under the bed and the twenty-minute phone calls and the four AM jjigae and the specific shape of a woman who takes care of everyone and lets no one take care of her. Love requires *depending*, and depending is the one thing Yu-jin has built her entire life around not doing, because the last time she depended on someone, he died in a warehouse and the system filed him away in seventy-two hours.

She closes the notebook.

She opens it again.

She writes: *I am afraid.*

It's the first time those words have appeared in the notebook. In eight years of contacts and favors and supply chains and Night Markets, she has never written those words.

She writes them now, in neat Korean, in the handwriting she inherited from her father.

그가 무서워. 사랑이 무서워.

*I'm afraid of him. I'm afraid of love.*

She stares at the words. Then, because she is her father's daughter and her father wrote everything down because the things you write down yourself, you remember:

하지만 혼자인 것도 무서워.

*But I'm afraid of being alone, too.*

***

### IX. Advice From An Old Lady

Mrs. Bae has been patient. Mrs. Bae has been restrained. Mrs. Bae has, by her own standards, been practically invisible in her orchestration of the emotional trajectory of two people who eat at her restaurant and fix her appliances and don't seem to understand that they are making everyone around them insane.

Mrs. Bae's patience ends on a Wednesday.

"Sit," Mrs. Bae says.

Yu-jin sits. There is jjigae. There is tea. There is the specific atmosphere of a woman who has decided that a conversation is going to happen and the universe will accommodate.

"How long are you going to do this?" Mrs. Bae asks.

"Do what?"

"This." She waves her ladle in a gesture that encompasses the entire restaurant, the lot outside where HAUS is parked, the apartment upstairs, the concept of human emotional avoidance, and possibly the fundamental nature of love in a hostile universe.

"I don't know what you're—"

"Yu-jin." Mrs. Bae sets down the ladle. This is significant. Mrs. Bae puts down the ladle the way other people put down weapons. "I watched your father for eleven years. He sat where you're sitting. He ordered the same thing every morning. He kept his notebooks. He was careful and quiet and he thought that being right would keep him safe."

"I know."

"You don't know. Because if you knew, you'd know that the thing your father got wrong wasn't trusting the system. It was trusting the system *instead of* the people."

Silence.

"Kang Tao didn't kill your father because he trusted them. They killed him because he was alone. No one watching his back. No crew." She points at Yu-jin with a finger that has sixty-eight years of authority behind it. "You have a crew. You have people who show up at two in the morning because they *want to*, not because you're paying them. And you have a man who drives three hours in the dark because you needed the road."

"Mrs. Bae—"

"I'm not finished." She is, demonstrably, not finished. "My husband was a fixer. You know this. What you don't know is that I almost lost him the same way — not to a job, but to his own stubbornness. He was so afraid of needing me that he almost destroyed the one thing that was keeping him alive." Her eyes, old and fierce and unutterably kind. "I didn't let him. I walked into his office and I told him the truth, and the truth was very simple: you are not less free because someone loves you. You are more free. Because freedom without someone to come home to isn't freedom. It's exile."

Yu-jin's hands are flat on the table. She can feel the grain of the wood. The warmth from the kitchen. The weight of eight years of independence pressing against something softer underneath.

"He might leave," Yu-jin says. "Everyone leaves."

"Everyone dies," Mrs. Bae corrects, gently. "That's not the same as leaving. Your father didn't leave. He was *taken*. And the man out there—" she points toward the lot where Deacon is working on HAUS, because Deacon is always working on HAUS — "that man lost his entire family and is still here. Still fixing things. Still showing up."

She picks up the ladle again. Conversation over.

"Now eat your soup. And for the love of everything sacred, go talk to him. Before I do."

***

## PART THREE — RETURN

### X. Confessions

She goes.

Not immediately. She eats her soup, because Mrs. Bae told her to and because you don't disobey Mrs. Bae. She finishes her tea. She closes the notebook. She walks outside.

It's early evening. The kind of Night City evening where the neon hasn't fully kicked in yet and the sky is doing something improbable with pinks and purples and the particular shade of chemical orange that passes for sunset in a city with seventeen active industrial zones.

Deacon is under HAUS's hood. Upper half visible, lower half Nomad-leather and engine grease. The charm swings in the window.

"Deacon."

He straightens up. Wrench in one hand, rag in the other. Grease on his forehead. The beard doing the thing it does where it makes him look like a man who belongs on a highway somewhere, heading toward a horizon he hasn't picked yet.

"Hey. Everything okay?"

"Can you stop working for a minute?"

He puts down the wrench. Wipes his hands on the rag. Gives her his full attention, the way he always does — immediately, completely, like there's nothing in the world more important than whatever she's about to say.

This is what breaks her.

Not the kindness. Not the jjigae. Not the three-hour drive or the dishwasher or the forty-minute trip to fix a lock she mentioned once. It's this: the attention. The way he is *here*, fully and completely here, and is not performing it or leveraging it or filing it for future reference. He's just here.

"I have something to tell you," she says. "And it's going to be — I'm going to do this badly. I want you to know in advance that I know I'm doing it badly."

"Okay."

"I have a notebook." She holds it up. The leather-bound, rubber-banded thing that contains the most valuable non-digital database in Watson. "I write everything down. Everyone I meet, everything they need, everything they owe, every connection I've ever brokered. It's my life's work. It's the most important thing I own."

"I know."

"There are four pages about you."

Deacon blinks.

"Not — not the business pages. I have a separate file for that. These are — other pages." She pushes her hair behind her left ear. She doesn't know she does this. She does it every time she's thinking hard enough to forget the protocols. "Observations. Details. Things I noticed because I can't stop noticing, because noticing is my pathology and you are—"

She stops. Breathes. Starts again.

"You fix things without being asked. You check on people. You brought me soup through Mrs. Bae because you heard I wasn't eating and you didn't even take credit for it. You drove for three hours because I needed the road and you didn't ask me to talk. You called me by my name." Her voice catches. "My *name*. Not Nabi. Not the Fixer. Yu-jin."

Deacon hasn't moved.

"I am afraid of you," she says. "Not because you're dangerous — because you're *safe*. And safe is the most terrifying thing in Night City, because safe things disappear. My father was safe. My family was safe. The systems were safe." She's talking faster now, the dam cracking. "I built my whole life around not needing anyone, because needing is how you get hurt, and I am very, *very* good at not needing—"

"Yu-jin."

"—but the truth is I've been cooking jjigae at two in the morning for a man who brings me food he won't take credit for, and I've been inventing reasons to inspect your cargo capacity, and I watched you fix a dishwasher and a lock and a step and an entire Night Market's lighting system, and at some point I realized that the reason I write everything down is so I don't forget, and the reason I have four pages about you is because I'm terrified of forgetting what this feels like—"

"Yu-jin."

"—and I know the timing is terrible and the logistics are impossible and we work together and there are at least four major geopolitical entities who want us dead for various reasons, but Mrs. Bae told me that freedom without someone to come home to is exile, and I think she might be right, and I—"

"*Yu-jin.*"

She stops.

Deacon is smiling. Not the polite smile, not the professional smile, not the one he uses with clients and contacts and the transactional world that runs on angles. This is the other one. The real one. The one that makes his eyes go warm and crinkled and stupid, the one she wrote about on page three of the four pages, the one she described as *"structurally compromising."*

"Can I say something?" he asks.

"… Yes."

"I fixed that dishwasher because I wanted to be in the same building as you. I fixed the step because you walk on it every morning and it was loose and the thought of you tripping made me unreasonable. I fixed the lock in Watson because you mentioned it once and I thought, if I fix it, she'll know someone was listening." He puts down the rag. "I drove three hours because you needed the road, and the road is all I have to give, but I'd give you every mile of it."

The neon is kicking in now. Pink and purple reflecting off HAUS's burnt orange hood. The charm swinging in the window. The city doing its city thing — loud and bright and indifferent to the two people standing in a parking lot having the most important conversation of their lives.

"I didn't know how to tell you," he says. "Nomads aren't — we show, we don't tell. We fix things. We show up. We make sure you've eaten." He takes a breath. "But I'm telling you now. I have been telling you for months, in every language I know except the one with words."

"Acts of service," she whispers.

"What?"

"Your love language. It's acts of service. Mine too. We've been—" She almost laughs. "We've been having an entire relationship in repairs and leftovers."

He does laugh. Full and warm and Southern-big, the kind of laugh that fills the space between two people who have been circling each other with the wary tenderness of a courtship neither of them knew was a courtship.

"So," he says. "Four pages?"

"Four pages."

"Can I read them?"

"Absolutely not."

"Fair enough." He steps closer. Close enough to be a question. "Can I — is this okay?"

She doesn't answer with words. She reaches up and wipes the grease off his forehead with her thumb. Slowly. Deliberately. With the precision of a woman who calculates the optimal angle of approach for every interaction and has decided that this one calls for tenderness.

He closes his eyes. Just for a second. Like the touch is something he's been waiting for without knowing he was waiting.

"It's okay," she says. Quiet. Sure. "It's okay."

He puts his arms around her. She puts her head against his chest. He smells like engine oil and HAUS's cab and the particular warmth of a person who has been working hard on something they care about.

It's not cinematic. It's not the rose ceremony. It's a woman and a man in a parking lot behind a noodle shop, holding each other while Night City hums and the neon flickers and Mrs. Bae watches from the kitchen window and doesn't say a single word, because some moments don't need an audience.

They just need to happen.

***

### XI. A Moon made of Honey

This is what love looks like in Night City:

It looks like two bowls of juk at Halmae's instead of one, set out every morning by Mrs. Bae with a look of ferocious satisfaction.

It looks like Deacon's jacket on the hook by Yu-jin's door, the one that smells like road dust and engine oil, the one she puts on when the apartment gets cold at night because it's warmer than any jacket she owns and also — but she will not say this out loud — because it smells like him.

It looks like the notebook. Specifically, the new section of the notebook — the one Yu-jin started after the parking lot, the one she writes in every night before bed. It's not observations anymore. It's not intelligence. It's the other kind of record — the kind her father kept, the kind that belongs to you and not the system.

*Tuesday: He made breakfast. Scrambled eggs, slightly burnt. He apologized for the burning. I told him burnt eggs are a Korean delicacy. He believed me for forty-five seconds. It was the best forty-five seconds of my week.*

*Thursday: He fell asleep on my couch watching Love in the Time of Chrome. I put a blanket over him. He mumbled "thank you, ma'am" in his sleep. My heart did something medically inadvisable.*

*Saturday: He introduced me to HAUS's charm. He held the braided cord and told me about his mother. I held his hand. He didn't cry. I did. He pretended not to notice. I pretended he was pretending. We are both very good at pretending, and getting worse at it daily.*

***

The crew reacts predictably.

Galen wins the betting pool. He donates the winnings to the Watson clinic, which is the most Galen thing that has ever happened.

Vincent updates his planner. He writes *CONFIRMED* next to the japchae frequency analysis. He then, in a rare display of human emotion, shakes Deacon's hand and says, "She's important. To the operational structure. And—" he pauses, recalibrates, "—to the crew. Don't be stupid."

"I'll do my best."

"Statistically, your best is adequate. But I'll be monitoring."

"I wouldn't expect anything less."

Troy is informed. Troy processes the information with the internal speed of a man whose emotional bandwidth was last updated in 2018. He says, "Is this going to affect operational readiness?"

"No, Troy."

"Will it introduce bias into mission-critical decisions?"

"Troy."

"I'm asking legitimate—"

"Troy. They're happy. That's the relevant datum."

A long pause. Troy's thumb twitches. "Good," he says finally, in a voice so quiet it's almost inaudible. "That's — good."

Mrs. Bae says nothing. She sets out two bowls every morning. She gives Deacon the counter seat. She watches them with the particular satisfaction of a woman who has been steering two people toward each other with the patient, invisible precision of a fixer's fixer.

***

The honeymoon period in Night City is not what the brochures suggest.

There are no weekend getaways. There is no brunch. There is a cargo container and a studio apartment and a noodle shop, and the love that happens in these spaces is built from the same materials as the city itself — imperfect, loud, occasionally on fire, and absolutely unwilling to die.

Deacon moves a spare jacket into Yu-jin's apartment. Then a toolkit. Then a photograph of HAUS's charm next to the photograph of her father on the wall. She doesn't comment on the progression. She makes room.

Yu-jin puts a shelf in the cargo container. A small one, near the workbench, where she sets a container of homemade kimchi that she replenishes every week. Deacon doesn't comment on the shelf. He eats the kimchi.

They fight about small things. He leaves engine parts on her kitchen table. She reorganizes his toolbox without asking, applying a filing system he doesn't understand but that is, objectively, more efficient. He says "thank you, ma'am" in contexts where "thank you, Yu-jin" would be more intimate, and she tells him this, and he says he'll work on it, and then he says "thank you, ma'am" again three minutes later, and she throws a dish towel at him, and he catches it, and they both laugh, and the fight is over before it starts because the fight was never real.

The real thing — the thing underneath the leftovers and the repairs and the dish towel — is this: two people who built their lives around self-sufficiency learning, slowly and with tremendous awkwardness, how to let someone in.

Deacon learns that Yu-jin cooks when she's stressed, and instead of asking what's wrong, he shows up in the kitchen and starts chopping vegetables. He's terrible at chopping vegetables. His technique is what a professional chef would call "violent." Yu-jin corrects his grip on the knife. Their hands overlap on the handle.

"Like this," she says.

"Yes, ma'am."

"What did I say about that?"

"Sorry. Yes, Yu-jin."

She doesn't correct the *yes*. She likes the yes. She likes the way he says her name like it means something.

Yu-jin learns that Deacon goes quiet when the clan comes back to him — when a radio frequency catches wrong or a convoy passes that looks too much like the ones he used to drive. She doesn't ask. She sits next to him in HAUS's cab and she opens the notebook and she works, and the sound of her pen on paper fills the silence the way a heartbeat fills a room, and after a while he reaches over and puts his hand on her knee, and she puts her hand over his, and neither of them says anything because nothing needs to be said.

***

### XII. Balance

Yu-jin calls her mother on a Sunday.

The call is twenty minutes, as always. They talk about Halmeoni's hip (healing). The pharmacy (busy). The weather in Busan (humid). The usual twenty minutes.

At minute nineteen, Yu-jin says: "I'm seeing someone."

Silence. The kind of silence that crosses twelve time zones.

"… Seeing someone?"

"A man. He's — he's a Nomad. He fixes things." She pauses. "He's good, eomma."

More silence. Then, very quietly:

"Does he eat properly?"

"He eats very properly. I make sure."

"Does he treat you well?"

"He drove me three hours down the coast at night because I was sad. He didn't ask me to talk."

"… That's very good."

"I know."

A long breath. Twelve time zones of held breath, exhaled.

"Your father would have liked a man who fixes things."

Yu-jin's throat closes. She swallows. Breathes.

"I think so too."

"Bring him to Busan."

"Eomma, we've been together for—"

"Bring him to Busan. Your grandmother will want to feed him."

The call goes to twenty-three minutes. It's the longest they've talked in years. It still ends with *"Eat properly. And call your halmeoni."*

But this time, something is different. This time, the silence at the end doesn't fill with the things they're not saying. It fills with something else.

It fills with enough.

***

### XIII. A Great Life

Here is the truth about the butterfly:

She was named Nabi because she moves between worlds without ever landing in any of them. Tyger Claws, Nomads, corporates, street kids — they all know her, none of them own her. This was the point. This was the design. A butterfly that lands belongs to the flower. A butterfly that flies belongs to no one.

But here is the other truth, the one that Campbell knew, the one that Mrs. Bae knew, the one that Yu-jin wrote in Korean on a page that no one will ever see:

The butterfly doesn't stop being a butterfly when she lands.

She stops being alone.

***

Evening in Japantown. The neon is up. The Night Market is humming. Mrs. Bae is threatening a Maelstrom ganger with a soup ladle. Galen is running the Watson clinic and making jokes that aren't jokes. Vincent is running overwatch from a rooftop somewhere, doing whatever Vincent does with his cybereyes and his threat models, which Yu-jin has decided she doesn't want to understand. Troy is somewhere being Troy, which involves a badge and a procedural commitment and a left thumb that twitches when someone says the word *feelings*.

And in a cargo container on the edge of the lot, in a space that smells like engine oil and kimchi and the particular warmth of two lives overlapping—

Yu-jin is sitting on the workbench, legs swinging, notebook open, pen moving. Deacon is under HAUS's hood, adjusting something that probably doesn't need adjusting but adjusts it anyway, because maintenance is love and love is maintenance and some things are the same thing.

"Deacon."

"Yeah?"

"I brought extra."

She holds out a container of japchae. The lids have their fingerprints layered over each other now, from months of this exact exchange.

He takes it. Pops the lid. Smiles.

"Thank you, Yu-jin."

Not ma'am. Not the Fixer. Not the butterfly.

Yu-jin.

She smiles back. The one with the crinkled eyes and the slight, knowing curve of the mouth. The one that means she's three moves ahead in a conversation you haven't started yet. The one that means she's here — all the way here — and she's not going anywhere.

She turns back to the notebook. Writes:

*It is a good life. I built it from nothing, with a pen and a dead man's methodology and a man who fixes things without being asked and a crew that showed up and a city that doesn't care about love but hosts it anyway, in cargo containers and noodle shops and the long road south where the stars get big enough to hold everything.*

*It is a good life. And it is not a small life. Not anymore.*

She closes the notebook. Looks up at the neon. Looks at Deacon.

He's watching her. He does that thing where he checks to make sure she's eaten before he eats. She does that thing where she pretends not to notice.

They both know. They've always known.

The butterfly has landed.

And she is free.

***

> *"The godly powers sought and dangerously won are revealed to have been within the heart of the hero all the time."*

## AUTHOR'S NOTES: NARRATIVE DESIGN

### Monomyth Mapping

This narrative loosely follows Joseph Campbell's hero's journey structure, reimagined as an emotional arc rather than a physical adventure:

| **Chapter**                       | **Campbell Stage**                   | **Narrative Equivalent**                                        |
| --------------------------------- | ------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------- |
| A Good Life                       | The Ordinary World                   | Yu-jin's independent, controlled life as a Fixer                |
| The Nomad                         | The Call to Adventure                | Meeting Deacon — encountering kindness she can't categorize     |
| I'm Fine                          | Refusal of the Call                  | Denying the attraction; independence as armor                   |
| Everyone Has Opinions             | Supernatural Aid                     | Mrs. Bae as mentor/guide figure                                 |
| I Really Wanna Stay At Your House | Crossing the First Threshold         | The 2 AM jjigae scene — first real vulnerability                |
| Everybody Notices                 | The Road of Trials                   | The slow burn courtship; the crew noticing                      |
| The Butterfly Lands               | The Innermost Cave                   | The grandmother's fall — confronting the fear of loss           |
| I Am Afraid                       | The Ordeal                           | Admitting love in the notebook; facing the fear of dependence   |
| Advice from an Old Lady           | Meeting with the Goddess / Atonement | Mrs. Bae's intervention — wisdom from the elder                 |
| Confessions/A Moon made of Honey  | The Ultimate Boon                    | The declaration; the honeymoon period                           |
| Balance                           | Master of Two Worlds                 | The phone call with her mother — integrating love into identity |
| A Great Life                      | Freedom to Live                      | The butterfly lands — independence AND belonging coexist        |

### Romance Framework

**Trope:** Friends to Lovers (professional relationship → friendship → love)

**Primary Conflict:** Internal — Yu-jin's fear of dependence, rooted in her father's death and the failure of systems she trusted. Deacon's complementary wound (losing his clan) makes him both the perfect partner and the greatest threat.

**Love Languages:** Both characters express love through Acts of Service. The comedy and tenderness come from two people having an *entire relationship in repairs and leftovers* without realizing it.

**Chemistry Source:** Deacon fulfills Yu-jin's deepest unmet need — being cared for by someone who doesn't want anything in return. Yu-jin fulfills Deacon's — being seen as home by someone who chooses to land.

### Cozy Romance Elements

**Honeymoon Emphasis:** Part Three lingers in the warmth of early love — the notebook entries, the small fights, the overlapping fingerprints on container lids.

**Low-Stakes Conflict:** Misunderstandings are charming (engine parts on the kitchen table, "thank you, ma'am"). The real conflict is internal and resolved through maturity and tenderness, not drama.

**Romantic Comedy:** The crew's betting pool, Galen's observations, Vincent's japchae frequency analysis, Troy's emotional processing speed.

**The Meddling Elder:** Mrs. Bae — the cozy romance staple of the wise older figure who sees what everyone else is too stubborn to see.

**Happily Ever After:** Required by genre, delivered through the final scene — the butterfly lands, and she is free.

**Re-Visitability:** Designed for re-reading through layered details, callbacks (the carburetor, the charm, the jjigae), and emotional beats that reward familiarity.

### GM Integration Hooks

This narrative is designed as a companion piece to Yu-jin's character dossier. Key moments can be deployed across campaign sessions:

- **The 2 AM Scene** can occur early in the campaign as a relationship-building beat
- **The Grandmother's Fall** provides a mid-campaign emotional crisis that isn't combat-related
- **Mrs. Bae's Intervention** offers a powerful NPC moment that deepens the world
- **The Declaration** should be earned across multiple sessions of slow-burn development
- **The Crew's Reactions** provide comic relief and character moments for all party members

The romance is a *slow burn that should develop over sessions, not scenes* — consistent with Yu-jin's dossier guidance. The narrative provides the complete arc; the GM's job is to space the beats across gameplay.
