## ORIGIN & ENVIRONMENT

Yu-jin Kim arrived in Night City at twelve years old, holding her father's hand and a suitcase that smelled like the sea.

Busan was salt air and container ships and her grandmother's apartment where the floor heating worked so well you could sleep on the kitchen tiles in January. Night City was none of those things. Night City was neon and concrete and a Japantown apartment so small her father slept on a folding mat in the kitchen while Yu-jin got the bedroom because he said growing girls needed doors that closed.

She didn't understand until later that he'd given her the only privacy in the apartment. At the time she just thought he liked the kitchen.

**Key traits of the environment:**

- Japantown operated on layered social currencies â€” language, cultural affiliation, and who-you-knew mattered more than money
- The Night Markets were ecosystems unto themselves â€” part bazaar, part intelligence exchange, part community center
- Trust was built slowly and broken instantly; reputation was the only credit line that didn't charge interest
- Information moved faster through noodle shop conversations than through any data network

Yu-jin learned early that the world ran on connections. Not the chrome-and-cable kind â€” the human kind. Who needed what. Who had what. Who owed whom, and how long they'd been owing. The people who thrived weren't the strongest or the smartest. They were the ones who understood that every person was simultaneously a customer, a supplier, and a story.

She decided to learn all three.

## THE MAKING OF A FIXER

After Tae-jun died, Yu-jin was nineteen, alone, and employed at a Japantown noodle shop called **Halmae's** â€” owned by an elderly Korean woman named **Bae Soon-hee** who everyone called Mrs. Bae or, if they were feeling brave, Auntie.

Kang Tao middle-managers who wanted to eat somewhere their expense reports couldn't track ate at Halmae's. Mrs. Bae knew all of them, remembered all of their orders, and understood that a restaurant in Japantown was not just a business â€” it was an information exchange with a soup menu.

Yu-jin watched. Yu-jin listened. Yu-jin started writing things down in a notebook.

**What Mrs. Bae Taught Her:**

- Every person who walks through a door is carrying two things: what they need and what they're afraid of. Learn both.
- Food is trust. People who eat together negotiate differently than people who don't.
- Never lie to a customer. If you can't give them what they want, tell them. They'll come back because you were honest. Lie to them once and they'll never come back at all.

**What Yu-jin Taught Herself:**

- The connections between people are more valuable than the people themselves. A solo who needs a ripperdoc and a ripperdoc who needs a bodyguard are two problems. Introduce them, and they're a solution. Solutions pay.
- Paper records can't be hacked. In a city where every data point is vulnerable to netrunners, a physical notebook is the most secure database in Night City.
- You don't need to be the strongest person in the room. You need to be the person everyone in the room needs.

It started small. A Watson clinic needed medical supplies; Yu-jin knew a Nomad crew with surplus trauma kits. A Tyger Claw lieutenant needed a discreet mechanic; Yu-jin knew a tech in Kabuki who didn't ask questions. A freelance solo needed steady work; Yu-jin knew three fixers who needed reliable muscle.

Every connection she brokered, she recorded. Every favor she facilitated, she tracked. Every debt owed to her â€” and every debt she owed â€” went into the notebook.

By twenty-two, she had a reputation. Not a loud one â€” loud reputations attract attention, and attention attracts complications. A quiet one. The Korean girl at Halmae's who could get you what you needed without getting you killed.

By twenty-five, she had a network. Watson, Japantown, the fringes of Pacifica, the Badlands border markets where Nomad crews sold surplus cargo. She organized Night Market events â€” pop-up bazaars where independent vendors could sell without gang interference, where medtechs could distribute supplies, where fixers could meet clients in neutral territory.

By twenty-seven, she had a handle. Nabi. The butterfly. Because she moved between worlds without ever quite landing in any of them â€” Tyger Claws, Nomads, corporates, street kids, all of them knew her, none of them owned her. 

She has been courted. Kang Tao's Night City division sent a recruiter with a salary offer and benefits package. Wakako Okada, the queen of Watson fixers, suggested a "mentorship arrangement" that was really a franchise proposal. A Tyger Claw oyabun offered "protection" in exchange for "priority consideration" on her supply chains.

Yu-jin turned them all down. Politely. Firmly. With exactly enough warmth that none of them felt insulted.

_"The moment you belong to someone,"_ she told the Tyger Claw lieutenant, _"you stop being useful to everyone. And I like being useful."_

The lieutenant considered this. Then he ordered the kimchi jjigae and didn't bring it up again.

## PERSONALITY

Yu-jin is warm the way a kitchen is warm â€” you don't notice it until you've been standing in it for ten minutes and realize you don't want to leave.

She doesn't care. Or rather â€” she cares about that, too. She cares that she doesn't care. It means the notebook isn't just a business ledger. It's a map of the people she's chosen to protect, and the map keeps getting bigger.

_"Kill me if you want,"_ she once told a Maelstrom ganger who was getting ideas about her inventory. _"But if that notebook disappears, forty people in this city lose their supply chains overnight. Including yours."_

He didn't kill her. He ordered the kimchi jjigae. Mrs. Bae charged him double.
